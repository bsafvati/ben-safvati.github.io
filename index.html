Research Background

I am a Physics PhD candidate at Stanford University working on the use of scanning tunneling microscopy (STM) to understand and manipulate quantum phases of matter. STM is an electronic imaging technique that can visualize material surfaces with atomic-scale spatial resolution using voltages applied from a metallic scanning tip. My research has focused on finding ways that STM and its many extensions can elucidate the many-body correlations that lead to novel and powerful electronic behavior in quantum materials. The interactions between the charge, spin, orbital, and topological degrees of freedom of electrons in these materials are unique in that they don't agree with existing solid-state theory and promise a new frontier for materials science and electronics, potentially in the same way that semiconductors revolutionized information technology in the last century.

In 2019 I completed my bachelor's degree in Physics from UC Berkeley, where I performed research in the Pines Lab using nitrogen-vacancy (NV) centers in diamond. These defects can be spin-polarized with optical radiation, and the goal of my work was to transfer this polarization to NMR-active carbon-13 nuclei in diamond and study the resulting spin dynamics and relaxation. I am still excited by the possibility of NV centers as broadly functional quantum sensors and their incorporation into hybrid quantum computing frameworks. Also at Berkeley, I developed an appreciation for the connections between machine learning and statistical physics, and chose to complete a senior thesis on this topic. I implemented a neural network variational ansatz for estimating ground states of spin chains, and evaluated its performance on a variety of model Hamiltonians with different spin, dimensionality, and interactions.
